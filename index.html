Case 1

A 62-year-old man arrives after a stroke. His speech is slow and effortful with a strained-strangled voice quality. His face droops on the right side only below the eye. He can wrinkle his forehead and close both eyes. When asked to stick out his tongue, it deviates to the right. His arms and legs show no weakness on either side.

Case 2

A 24-year-old woman sustained a cervical spinal cord injury at C4 in a car accident. She can speak but her voice is quiet and breathy. She runs out of air after four or five words and has to take frequent breaths mid-sentence. Her face and tongue move normally, and her articulation is clear when she has enough air.

Case 3

A 71-year-old man with Parkinson's disease has a stooped, forward-flexed posture. His speech is monopitched and monoloud with reduced vocal intensity. He does not have facial droop, tongue deviation, or articulatory imprecision. His voice quality is breathy and weak, but his larynx itself shows no structural abnormality on exam.

Case 4

A 58-year-old woman has suffered a series of small bilateral strokes. Her face shows little expression. Her speech is extremely slow, nasal, and effortful. When she tries to say "pataka" it comes out forced and imprecise. Her tongue moves sluggishly and cannot be protruded quickly. Her jaw jerk reflex is exaggerated. She laughs and cries at inappropriate times.

Case 5

A neurology resident is testing a patient with a suspected brainstem lesion. The patient's left arm and left leg are weak. The right side of his tongue deviates upon protrusion. Limb weakness and tongue deviation are on opposite sides of the body. Where could a single lesion produce this crossed pattern?

Case 6

A researcher is studying gesture-speech coupling. A participant is asked to make sharp downward arm movements while sustaining a vowel. The researcher notices the vowel's amplitude spikes at the exact moment the arm decelerates. Which system is responsible for the limb movement creating this acoustic effect?
