▶ Case 1

A 62-year-old man arrives after a stroke. His speech is slow and effortful with a strained-strangled voice quality. His face droops on the right side only below the eye. He can wrinkle his forehead and close both eyes. When asked to stick out his tongue, it deviates to the right. His arms and legs show no weakness on either side.

▶ Case 2

A 24-year-old woman sustained a cervical spinal cord injury at C4 in a car accident. She can speak but her voice is quiet and breathy. She runs out of air after four or five words and has to take frequent breaths mid-sentence. Her face and tongue move normally, and her articulation is clear when she has enough air.

▶ Case 3

A 71-year-old man with Parkinson's disease has a stooped, forward-flexed posture. His speech is monopitched and monoloud with reduced vocal intensity. He does not have facial droop, tongue deviation, or articulatory imprecision. His voice quality is breathy and weak, but his larynx itself shows no structural abnormality on exam.
